# First 30 days: quick-win cyber security actions

This 30‑day plan gives non‑security analysts a clear sequence of actions to reduce cyber risk in a small or medium business.

The goal is not to “solve security” in one month. The goal is to establish basic hygiene: know what you have, secure accounts and devices, enable updates and backups, and make people more aware.

You can adapt this plan to your organization. If a step does not apply, skip it. If you already do something, quickly verify that it is working as intended.

---

## Week 1 – Understand what you have

**Objective:** Build a simple picture of your environment so that later controls are not blind.

1. List your devices  
   - Include laptops, desktops, servers, smartphones and tablets used for business.  
   - Note who uses each device and whether it is company-owned or personal (bring‑your‑own).

2. List your key systems and applications  
   - Examples: email, file sharing, finance/accounting, CRM, HR systems, line-of-business applications.  
   - For each system, write down who the vendor is and how users access it (browser, installed app, remote access, etc.).

3. Identify important data  
   - Customer data, financial records, employee information, intellectual property and other sensitive information.  
   - Note where this data is stored (cloud services, local servers, laptops, external drives).

4. Identify who is responsible  
   - For each system or major data set, write down an internal “owner” (for example, finance manager owns the accounting system, HR owns HR data).  
   - This does not need to be perfect; the goal is to make accountability visible.

You can do this in a simple spreadsheet or table. Keep it short and practical.

---

## Week 2 – Secure accounts and devices

**Objective:** Reduce the chance that attackers can easily log in using weak or stolen credentials.

1. Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA)  
   - Turn on MFA for email accounts and key business systems wherever the option is available.  
   - Start with administrators and privileged accounts, then extend to all users.

2. Stop using shared generic accounts where possible  
   - Give each person their own named account instead of generic logins like “admin” or “sales”.  
   - If you must use shared accounts, limit them and protect them with strong credentials and MFA.

3. Enforce strong passwords or passphrases  
   - Use long passphrases (for example, several random words) or strong passwords generated by a password manager.  
   - Do not reuse the same password across different systems, especially for email and financial systems.

4. Protect administrator accounts  
   - Identify who has admin access to systems and devices.  
   - Make sure their accounts have MFA, strong passwords and are not used for everyday activities when a normal account would be enough.

5. Basic device hardening  
   - Require a screen lock or password/PIN on laptops, desktops and mobile devices.  
   - Set devices to automatically lock after a short period of inactivity.

---

## Week 3 – Updates and backups

**Objective:** Make it harder for attackers to exploit known vulnerabilities and reduce impact if something goes wrong.

1. Turn on automatic updates where practical  
   - Enable automatic updates for operating systems (Windows, macOS, mobile OS) and commonly used applications (browsers, office suites, security software).  
   - For servers or critical systems, define a simple process to apply updates regularly and test basic functionality afterwards.

2. Verify security software  
   - Make sure every business device has reputable antivirus/anti‑malware software installed and running.  
   - Confirm that the software is updating its signatures automatically.

3. Establish at least one working backup  
   - Identify critical data that must be backed up (for example, file shares, key databases, important laptops).  
   - Set up at least one backup solution (cloud backup or external drive) that runs regularly.

4. Test a small restore  
   - Do not assume backups are working. Try restoring at least one file to confirm you can recover data.  
   - Document in one or two sentences how to start a restore and who is responsible.

---

## Week 4 – People and basic awareness

**Objective:** Make people less likely to fall for common attacks and ensure they know what to do when something looks wrong.

1. Send a short awareness message  
   - Explain, in simple terms, the main signs of phishing emails (unexpected attachments, urgent language, requests for passwords or payments, suspicious links).  
   - Remind staff not to open unexpected attachments or click on links from unknown senders.

2. Define a “when in doubt” rule  
   - For example: “If you are not sure about an email or link, do not click. Forward it to IT or your manager and ask.”  
   - Make it clear that asking is encouraged and people will not be blamed for reporting suspicious messages.

3. Define a basic incident contact  
   - Decide who should be contacted first if someone suspects an incident (for example, IT, a specific manager, or an external support provider).  
   - Share this contact information in a short message or internal document.

4. Capture what you have done  
   - At the end of the 30 days, write a short summary of what was completed, what is in progress and what still needs work.  
   - This summary will help you plan the next 3–6 months and explain progress to management.

---

## After the first 30 days

After completing (or partially completing) this plan, the next steps are to:

- Formalize simple policies (for example, acceptable use, password and MFA, backup and restoration, remote work).  
- Implement more detailed controls from the `3-practical-controls/` section.  
- Align your actions with recognized frameworks, such as the NIST Cybersecurity Framework and CIS Critical Security Controls, as described in `4-frameworks-explained/`.[web:19][web:22][web:5][web:17]

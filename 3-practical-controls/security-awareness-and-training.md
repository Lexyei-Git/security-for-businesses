# Security awareness and training

Technology alone cannot protect a business. People make decisions every day that affect security: which links to click, where to store data, how to handle suspicious messages, and when to ask for help.

Security awareness and training are about **helping people make safer choices**, not turning everyone into security experts.

---

## 1. Goals of security awareness for SMBs

For small and medium businesses, a realistic awareness program should:

- Focus on a **small number of high‑impact topics** (for example, phishing, passwords/MFA, device and data handling, incident reporting).  
- Use **short, practical content**, not long lectures.  
- Be **ongoing**, not a one‑time presentation.  
- Integrate with onboarding and regular communications.

The objective is to change day‑to‑day behavior just enough to reduce common risks, especially phishing, account compromise and accidental data exposure.

---

## 2. Core topics to cover

For most SMBs, the following topics give the best return on effort:

1. **Phishing and social engineering**
   - How to recognize suspicious emails and messages.  
   - What to do (and not do) when something looks wrong.

2. **Passwords and MFA**
   - Why strong, unique passwords/passphrases and MFA matter.  
   - Simple rules for creating and managing them.

3. **Device and data handling**
   - Locking screens, storing data in approved locations, handling paper records.  
   - What to do if a device is lost or stolen.

4. **Incident reporting**
   - What counts as a security incident.  
   - How and when to report (and that early reporting is encouraged).

5. **Acceptable use and basic privacy**
   - Appropriate use of company systems and data.  
   - Respect for customer and employee information.

These topics align directly with the practical controls and policies in this repository.

---

## 3. Simple annual awareness plan

You do not need an expensive training platform to start. A basic plan for one year might look like this:

- **Onboarding**
  - New starters receive:
    - A short introduction to key policies (acceptable use, passwords/MFA, email/phishing, incident reporting).  
    - Links to this guide (or the relevant extracts) and contacts for questions.

- **Quarterly focus topics**
  - Q1 – Phishing and email safety.  
  - Q2 – Passwords, MFA and account security.  
  - Q3 – Device and data handling (including remote work).  
  - Q4 – Incident reporting, lessons learned and end‑of‑year recap.

For each quarter, deliver **at least one** of:

- A short (15–30 minute) talk or virtual session.  
- An internal newsletter or email with concrete examples.  
- A quick quiz or survey to reinforce key points.

Keep materials **short and practical**, with examples that reflect your environment.

---

## 4. Using real examples from your own environment

Real (anonymized) incidents and near‑misses are powerful teaching tools:

- Collect examples of:
  - Phishing emails received by staff (with sensitive details removed).  
  - Common mistakes (for example, sending an email to the wrong recipient, misconfigured file sharing).  
  - Real incidents or alerts that could have been worse.

- Turn them into:
  - Screenshots with red circles around the warning signs.  
  - Short “What would you do?” scenarios.  
  - One‑page “lessons learned” summaries.

People learn better when they see familiar tools (your actual email client, your login screens) rather than generic stock examples.

---

## 5. Phishing simulations (optional but helpful)

If you have access to a phishing simulation tool (some email platforms or security solutions include this), you can:

- Run **occasional, simple simulations**:
  - Start with obvious examples to build awareness and avoid shaming staff.  
  - Use results to highlight trends, not to punish individuals.

- Use simulations to:
  - Identify where additional training is needed.  
  - Show improvement over time.

If you do not have such tools, focus on training with real examples and reinforce the habit of reporting suspicious messages.

---

## 6. Integrating awareness with policies and controls

Awareness should reinforce, not replace, your policies and controls:

- Tie training directly to existing documents:
  - Acceptable use → how to use devices and systems responsibly.  
  - Passwords and MFA policy → how to protect accounts.  
  - Backup and data protection policy → why saving data in approved locations matters.  
  - Incident reporting policy → what to do when something goes wrong.

- Reference relevant **practical controls** in this repository:
  - `email-and-phishing.md` during phishing training.  
  - `passwords-and-mfa.md` for account security sessions.  
  - `physical-security-basics.md` when discussing device handling.

This helps staff see awareness as part of a coherent whole, not a random set of rules.

---

## 7. Roles and responsibilities

Even in a small organization, it helps to assign responsibilities for awareness:

- **Security/IT lead or champion**
  - Owns the awareness plan.  
  - Coordinates topics, materials and delivery.

- **Managers**
  - Reinforce messages in team meetings.  
  - Make time for staff to attend short sessions or complete quick modules.

- **HR**
  - Integrates key topics into onboarding and periodic HR communications.

- **All staff**
  - Participate in training and apply what they learn.  
  - Ask questions when something is unclear.  
  - Report suspected incidents quickly.

---

## 8. Measuring effectiveness (lightweight)

You do not need complex metrics, but some measurement helps show progress:

Possible indicators:

- **Activity metrics**
  - Number of awareness activities delivered (sessions, newsletters, simulations).  
  - Percentage of staff who attended or acknowledged key training.

- **Behavioral indicators**
  - Increase in timely incident reports and questions about suspicious messages.  
  - Reduction in repeat simple mistakes (for example, repeated clickers in simulations, if used).

- **Outcome indicators**
  - Trends in phishing or malware incidents (frequency, impact).  
  - Faster reporting and response times.

The goal is to demonstrate that awareness is changing behavior, not just ticking a checkbox.

---

## 9. Minimal checklist for security awareness and training

Use this checklist to evaluate your current state:

- [ ] Security awareness is part of **onboarding** for all new staff.  
- [ ] We have identified a **small set of core topics** and cover them at least once per year.  
- [ ] We use **real examples** from our own environment in training and communications.  
- [ ] Staff know how to recognize and report suspicious emails and incidents.  
- [ ] Managers and HR support awareness activities and allocate time for them.  
- [ ] We track basic participation (who attended or completed training).  
- [ ] We review and update our awareness plan at least annually based on incidents and feedback.

If several boxes are unchecked, use this document to design a lightweight but effective awareness program over the next 6–12 months.

# Physical security basics

Physical security is about protecting your people, premises and equipment from theft, damage and unauthorized access.

For small and medium businesses, basic physical controls support cyber security by preventing attackers from simply walking in, stealing devices, or accessing systems directly.

---

## 1. Why physical security matters for cyber security

Many cyber incidents start with a physical weakness, for example:

- A stolen laptop with unencrypted data.  
- An intruder connecting a device to your network.  
- Someone following an employee into a restricted area (“tailgating”).  
- Paper records containing sensitive information left in public view or discarded without shredding.

Physical controls:

- Protect devices and infrastructure that store or process sensitive data.  
- Reduce opportunities for insider threats and accidental exposure.  
- Support compliance requirements and customer expectations.

---

## 2. Access to premises and sensitive areas

You do not need a complex badge system to improve physical access control, but you should have **clear rules** about who can go where.

Basic measures:

- **Controlled entry**
  - Keep external doors closed and locked when appropriate.  
  - Use access control (keys, key cards, codes) for staff‑only areas.  
  - Avoid using a single “master key” without backup or tracking.

- **Zones and sensitive areas**
  - Identify areas that store or process sensitive information (for example, server rooms, network closets, records storage).  
  - Restrict access to these areas to authorized personnel only.  
  - Keep network equipment (for example, switches, routers, firewalls) in locked cabinets or rooms.

- **Visitor management**
  - Have a simple procedure for visitors:
    - Sign‑in at reception or in a log.  
    - Wear visitor badges where appropriate.  
    - Be escorted in non‑public areas.

- **Tailgating awareness**
  - Ask staff not to let unknown people follow them through locked doors without verifying who they are and why they are there.

---

## 3. Protecting devices and media

Endpoints, servers and removable media are common targets for theft or misuse.

Key practices:

- **Laptops and mobile devices**
  - Use cable locks or secure storage (drawer, cabinet) when devices are left unattended in the office.  
  - Avoid leaving laptops in visible places in vehicles; if unavoidable, lock them out of sight.  
  - Enable full‑disk encryption so that, if a device is stolen, data is harder to access.

- **Servers and network equipment**
  - Install in locked rooms or racks; do not leave them in open areas or under desks.  
  - Limit physical keys or access tokens for these locations and track who has them.

- **Portable media**
  - Minimize the use of USB drives and external hard drives for sensitive data.  
  - Use encrypted storage devices where portable media are necessary.  
  - Establish a simple policy for how to handle and dispose of old media (for example, wiping or physical destruction).

- **Paper records**
  - Store sensitive documents in locked cabinets.  
  - Use “clean desk” practices where sensitive information is not left in plain view.  
  - Shred or securely destroy confidential documents instead of throwing them in regular trash.

---

## 4. Deterrence and detection: cameras, lighting and alarms

Physical security is stronger when you combine **deterrence** (making attacks less attractive) and **detection** (knowing when something happens).

Examples:

- **Lighting**
  - Ensure exterior doors, parking areas and loading bays are well‑lit.  
  - Consider motion‑activated lighting around entrances and vulnerable areas.

- **Locks and barriers**
  - Use commercial‑grade locks for entry points and sensitive rooms.  
  - Secure windows and secondary entrances where practical.

- **Cameras and surveillance**
  - Install cameras to monitor entrances, critical interior spaces and areas with valuable assets.  
  - Ensure footage is stored reliably and retained for an appropriate period.  
  - Restrict access to live feeds and recordings to authorized staff.

- **Alarms and sensors**
  - Use alarm systems on doors and windows, especially outside business hours.  
  - Consider glass‑break or motion sensors for key areas.  
  - Ensure alarms are monitored or configured to notify designated contacts.

You do not need every technology at once; prioritize according to your risk profile and budget.

---

## 5. Staff awareness and procedures

People are central to physical security. Even simple procedures can significantly reduce risk.

Suggested practices:

- **Opening and closing procedures**
  - Document and train staff on how to open and close the premises, including:
    - Which doors to lock.  
    - How to arm or disarm alarm systems.  
    - How to check that sensitive areas are secured.

- **Key and card management**
  - Keep a record of who has which keys or access cards.  
  - Have a process for revoking access when staff leave or change roles.  
  - Update alarm codes and access rights when keys or codes are lost or staff depart.

- **Reporting suspicious activity**
  - Encourage staff to report suspicious behaviour (for example, unknown individuals in restricted areas, doors propped open, missing equipment).  
  - Make it clear who they should contact and that raising concerns is encouraged.

- **Training**
  - Include basic physical security expectations in onboarding (locking screens, securing devices, visitor handling).  
  - Periodically remind staff of key points, especially after incidents or near‑misses.

---

## 6. Physical security and incident response

Physical incidents (for example, theft, break‑in, lost devices) should be integrated into your broader incident response process.

Key steps:

- **Immediate actions**
  - Ensure people are safe first.  
  - Secure the area if appropriate and safe to do so.  
  - Report theft or break‑ins to law enforcement as required.

- **Cyber implications**
  - For lost or stolen devices:
    - Remotely lock or wipe the device if possible.  
    - Change passwords and revoke access tokens associated with the device.  
    - Review logs for suspicious access from the lost device.

- **Evidence and documentation**
  - Preserve relevant camera footage, access logs and alarm records.  
  - Document what happened, what was affected and what actions were taken.

- **Lessons learned**
  - After an incident, review:
    - Which physical controls worked or failed.  
    - Whether locks, alarms, cameras or procedures should be improved.  
    - Whether staff need additional guidance.

---

## 7. Minimal checklist for physical security

Use this checklist as a quick status check:

- [ ] External doors and sensitive internal areas are controlled (locked and accessible only to authorized staff).  
- [ ] Network equipment and servers are kept in locked rooms or cabinets, not in public or easily accessible spaces.  
- [ ] Laptops and portable devices are protected (cable locks, secure storage, encryption) and not left unattended in public or visible in vehicles.  
- [ ] There is a clear process for managing keys, access cards and alarm codes, including revocation when staff leave.  
- [ ] Sensitive paper records are stored securely and shredded or securely destroyed when no longer needed.  
- [ ] Exterior areas and entrances are adequately lit; cameras and/or alarms are used where risk justifies it.  
- [ ] Staff are trained on basic physical security procedures (opening and closing, visitors, reporting suspicious activity).  
- [ ] Physical incidents (for example, theft, break‑ins, lost devices) are included in the organization’s incident response procedures.

Unchecked boxes highlight areas where you can strengthen physical security over the next 30–90 days.
